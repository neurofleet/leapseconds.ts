Future Outlook For This Library

Timekeeping policy is shifting, but the core risk profile for this table-driven implementation is still favorable.

  • Earth rotation is variable (it can speed up and slow down), so leap-second pressure can change direction.
  • A negative leap second is possible and has never been executed in production UTC.
  • A 2024 peer-reviewed analysis projects UTC could need its first negative step by around 2029 if current rotation trends continue.
  • Current policy work targets increasing the allowed UT1-UTC bound by or before 2035, with the next formal CGPM step in 2026.
  • Practical implication for this code: likely zero or very few future table updates, with a low-probability scenario of one negative step before policy transition.

As of 2026-01-06 (IERS Bulletin C 71): no leap second is scheduled at the end of June 2026.

Sources: IERS Bulletin C 71, NIST Leap Seconds FAQ, CGPM Resolution 4 (2022), Nature 2024 (Agnew).

Why NTP/POSIX Fails for Swarm SLAM

Swarm SLAM needs durable, cross-platform timestamps that stay comparable over years and across data types. POSIX/NTP civil time is not a final canonical format for that job.

  • Long-horizon correlation suffers when clocks are stepped/smeared differently across hosts.
  • Astronomy, ephemeris, and GPS feeds require explicit leap-second handling for correct UTC alignment.
  • No new leap second has occurred since 2017-01-01, so many stacks are operationally "cold" on leap events.
  • A first-ever negative leap second remains possible, so discontinuity handling should be treated as a safety case, not an edge case.
  • Canonical storage in TAI/GPS-like monotonic time reduces ambiguity and improves replayability.
